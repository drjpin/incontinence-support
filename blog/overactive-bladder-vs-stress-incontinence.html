Overactive Bladder vs. Stress Incontinence: What's the Difference — and Why It Matters

April 15, 2026 • 9 min read • By Dr. Joshua Fritz, DC

Bladder leaks aren't all the same — and treating the wrong type won't work. Stress incontinence and overactive bladder have different causes, different triggers, and need different approaches. Here's how to tell which one you have.

If you've been told "you have incontinence" and sent home with generic advice that hasn't helped, there's a good reason it's not working. Incontinence is not a single condition. The two most common types — stress incontinence and overactive bladder — have completely different underlying mechanisms, different triggers, and different solutions. A treatment that works well for one type can do almost nothing for the other. Knowing which type you have isn't just useful information — it's the difference between getting better and spinning your wheels for months.

Stress Incontinence — The "Movement Leak"

Stress incontinence is a mechanical problem. The pelvic floor muscles and urethral sphincter — the structures responsible for keeping the urethra sealed — are not strong enough to resist sudden increases in abdominal pressure. When that pressure spikes, it overcomes the sphincter's ability to stay closed, and urine leaks out.[1]

Critically, the bladder itself is not the problem. The bladder is passive — it's not contracting, it's not sending an "urgent" signal. The pressure is simply overwhelming a weakened seal. Think of it like a garden hose with a worn washer: if you suddenly increase the water pressure, it leaks at the fitting — not because the hose wants to empty, but because the seal can't hold.

Common triggers for stress incontinence:

  • Coughing (including chronic cough from allergies or asthma)
  • Sneezing
  • Laughing hard
  • Jumping, running, or high-impact exercise
  • Lifting heavy objects
  • Getting up quickly from a seated or lying position

Who tends to develop stress incontinence:

  • Women after vaginal childbirth — particularly after multiple births or large babies — where the pelvic floor muscles and connective tissue are stretched and sometimes torn
  • Women during perimenopause and menopause, when declining estrogen weakens pelvic floor muscle tone and urethral tissue
  • Athletes in high-impact sports (gymnastics, running, CrossFit) where repeated abdominal pressure loads the pelvic floor over years
  • People with chronic cough from any cause

The key distinguishing feature: leakage happens at the exact moment of the trigger — you cough and you leak simultaneously. There's no warning, no urge beforehand. The leak is directly tied to the pressure event.

Overactive Bladder (Urge Incontinence) — The "Can't Wait" Type

Overactive bladder (OAB) is a neurological and muscular problem, not a structural one. The detrusor — the smooth muscle that forms the wall of the bladder — contracts involuntarily before the bladder is actually full. The brain-bladder signaling system misfires, sending urgent "go now" signals regardless of how much urine is actually present.[2]

When the detrusor contracts unexpectedly and strongly enough, the sphincter can't hold against an active bladder contraction, and leakage occurs — this is urge incontinence, also called "OAB wet." When the urgency is intense but leakage doesn't occur, it's called "OAB dry." Both are OAB; the wet version simply means the bladder contraction is winning against the sphincter before the person can reach the bathroom.

Symptoms of overactive bladder:

  • Sudden, intense urge to urinate that's hard to suppress
  • Leaking on the way to the bathroom — the urge comes first, leakage follows
  • Urinating 8 or more times per day (frequent small voids)
  • Nocturia — waking one or more times at night to urinate
  • Urges triggered by specific cues: hearing running water, going outside in cold air, putting the key in the front door ("latchkey urgency"), pulling into the driveway

That last point is worth noting. OAB urges are often conditioned responses — the brain has learned to associate certain cues with urination, and starts firing the detrusor in anticipation. This is why bladder retraining, which interrupts these conditioned patterns, is a cornerstone of OAB treatment.

The key distinguishing feature: you feel a strong urge before any leakage occurs. You know you need to go, you're rushing to get there, and you may or may not make it. Physical movement itself does not cause the leak — the urgency and the bladder contraction precede it.

Mixed Incontinence — Both at Once

Mixed incontinence means you have both stress incontinence and overactive bladder simultaneously — and it is very common. Studies suggest that mixed incontinence may affect as many women as either pure type alone, particularly in menopausal women and women who have had bladder symptoms for years.

Both mechanisms are active at the same time: the pelvic floor is weak enough that pressure leaks occur with activity, and the detrusor is overactive enough to produce urgency episodes as well. The experience is often confusing — leaks happen both with movement and with urgency, and it's not always clear which type is responsible for which episode.

In most cases, one component is dominant. You might have frequent urgency episodes with occasional stress leaks, or mostly stress leaks with occasional urgency. Identifying which is dominant helps prioritize treatment. Both components need to be addressed for full resolution, but starting with the more disruptive type often produces the most immediate relief.

Quick Self-Check — Which Type Do You Have?

Review the symptoms below. This isn't a medical diagnosis, but it can help you identify which type is most likely driving your symptoms.

Stress incontinence indicators:

  • You leak when you cough, sneeze, or laugh
  • You leak during exercise, running, jumping, or lifting
  • Leakage happens at the same moment as the physical trigger — no warning
  • Leaks tend to be small in volume (a few drops to a small squirt)
  • You don't feel any urgency before the leak — it just happens
  • You rarely leak at night or when you're not moving

Overactive bladder indicators:

  • You get a strong, sudden urge to urinate that's hard to ignore or delay
  • You urinate 8 or more times per day
  • You wake at night to urinate (nocturia)
  • You sometimes leak on the way to the bathroom
  • Urges are triggered by cold air, running water, arriving home, or other specific cues
  • Leaks can be larger in volume when they occur

Mixed incontinence: If you checked items from both lists, you likely have mixed incontinence. This is extremely common — don't be surprised if both apply to you.

Other Types Worth Knowing

Stress incontinence and OAB account for the vast majority of incontinence in adults, but two other types are worth brief mention:

Overflow incontinence occurs when the bladder never fully empties. Urine accumulates until pressure forces it out in small, frequent dribbles. This is more common in men with prostate enlargement that obstructs urine flow, but can also occur with nerve damage (from diabetes, spinal cord injury, or pelvic surgery) that impairs the bladder's ability to contract. If you feel like your bladder never empties completely, or you have a weak, dribbling stream, this warrants a doctor visit.

Functional incontinence occurs when the bladder and sphincter are working normally, but a physical or cognitive barrier prevents reaching the toilet in time — severe mobility limitation, dementia, or being on medications that impair alertness. The plumbing works; the problem is access or awareness. This is common in older adults in care settings.

Both overflow and functional incontinence respond poorly to pelvic floor therapies and typically require medical evaluation to identify and treat the underlying cause.

Why the Distinction Matters for Treatment

This is where getting the diagnosis right becomes critical. The treatments that work for stress incontinence and OAB are fundamentally different — and applying the wrong one wastes time.

Stress incontinence is a pelvic floor muscle problem. The sphincter and supporting muscles lack the strength and coordination to resist abdominal pressure spikes. Treatment needs to build that strength. This means:

  • Pelvic floor muscle training (Kegels done correctly, or Emsella)
  • Pelvic floor physical therapy with biofeedback
  • Addressing contributing factors like chronic cough or abdominal obesity
  • In severe cases: surgical options like a urethral sling

Overactive bladder is a bladder muscle and nerve signaling problem. The detrusor is hyperactive — it contracts when it shouldn't. Treatment needs to calm or retrain that reflex. This means:

  • Bladder retraining — gradually extending the intervals between bathroom trips to retrain the urgency signal
  • Urge suppression techniques — interrupting the conditioned urgency response before it triggers a contraction
  • Reducing bladder irritants (caffeine, alcohol, artificial sweeteners, acidic foods)
  • Medications: anticholinergics or beta-3 agonists to reduce detrusor overactivity
  • Nerve stimulation therapies to modulate the detrusor reflex

Here's the practical implication: Kegels help stress incontinence but do almost nothing for OAB. Strengthening the pelvic floor does not fix a bladder that fires involuntarily. Conversely, bladder retraining and medications help OAB but do nothing to address the muscle weakness underlying stress incontinence. If you have stress incontinence and spend months retraining your bladder, you'll be frustrated. If you have OAB and spend months doing Kegels, you'll be equally frustrated. Identifying the type first is not a minor detail.

How Emsella Treats Both Types

Most treatments target one mechanism or the other. Emsella is unusual in that it addresses both — which is why it's particularly effective for mixed incontinence and why its clinical success rate of 95% holds across both types.[3]

For stress incontinence: Emsella uses High-Intensity Focused Electromagnetic (HIFEM) technology to trigger approximately 11,200 pelvic floor contractions in a single 28-minute session. This level of neuromuscular activation is not achievable through voluntary exercise — no matter how many Kegels you do. The muscle fibers are recruited and fatigued at a depth and intensity that voluntary contraction cannot replicate. The result is a substantially stronger pelvic floor and a urethral sphincter with the holding power to resist abdominal pressure spikes.

For overactive bladder: The electromagnetic stimulation doesn't just activate pelvic floor muscles — it also stimulates the pudendal nerve, the primary nerve governing bladder and pelvic floor function. This neuromodulation calms the overactive detrusor reflex, reducing the involuntary contractions responsible for urgency and urge leakage. The mechanism is similar in principle to sacral nerve stimulation (an implanted device used for OAB), but Emsella achieves it non-invasively, with no surgery, no implant, and no recovery time.

For mixed incontinence: Because Emsella works on both the muscular and neurological components simultaneously, patients with mixed incontinence often respond very well. Both the stress leak component and the urgency component are being addressed in the same session.

It's still worth knowing your type even if you're pursuing Emsella treatment. Tracking which symptoms improve first — and how quickly — gives useful information about which component is dominant. Patients with primarily stress incontinence often notice improvement earlier in the treatment series, while OAB improvement can take slightly longer as the neuromodulation effect builds over the course of treatment.

When to See a Doctor

The self-check above can help you understand your symptoms, but a clinical diagnosis is more accurate and sometimes necessary. A urogynecologist or urologist can perform urodynamic testing — a specialized set of measurements that directly evaluates bladder pressure, sphincter function, and detrusor activity — to definitively identify which type or types are present and how severe they are. This is particularly useful if your symptoms are complex, mixed, or have not responded to initial treatment.

See a doctor promptly (not after trying self-directed treatment for months) if any of the following apply:

  • Your symptoms came on suddenly rather than gradually
  • There is blood in your urine
  • You have pain in your bladder, pelvis, or when urinating
  • Your symptoms are getting rapidly worse over weeks
  • You suspect overflow incontinence — a feeling of incomplete emptying, weak stream, or constant dribbling
  • You have neurological symptoms (numbness, weakness, or tingling in the legs) alongside bladder changes

These features can indicate infections, bladder pathology, or neurological conditions that need to be ruled out before pursuing pelvic floor therapy.

The Takeaway

Stress incontinence and overactive bladder feel different, have different causes, and require different treatments. Stress incontinence is about a pelvic floor that can't hold against pressure; OAB is about a bladder that fires when it shouldn't. Mixed incontinence — having both — is common and very treatable. Knowing which type (or combination) is driving your symptoms is the foundation of getting effective treatment rather than months of trial and error.

If your current approach isn't working, the most important question to ask is: am I treating the right type?
