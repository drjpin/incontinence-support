Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM): The Condition Behind Your Bladder Leaks, Vaginal Dryness, and Pelvic Discomfort

April 22, 2026 • 10 min read • By Dr. Joshua Fritz, DC

Most women going through menopause know about hot flashes. Far fewer have heard of GSM — the clinical term for a constellation of urogenital changes that causes bladder leaks, vaginal dryness, urgency, recurrent UTIs, and pelvic discomfort. It affects the majority of postmenopausal women. It rarely gets better on its own. And it's very treatable.

Ask most people to name a menopause symptom, and they'll say hot flashes. Ask about GSM, and you'll usually get a blank stare — even from women who are living with it right now. Yet genitourinary syndrome of menopause affects up to 84% of postmenopausal women,[1] and unlike hot flashes — which often diminish over time — GSM typically gets worse without treatment. The tissues involved don't stabilize. They continue to change.

GSM has a name. It has a mechanism. It has effective treatments that remain dramatically underused. And the millions of women quietly managing bladder leaks, vaginal discomfort, and recurrent UTIs deserve to know that these aren't just inevitable consequences of aging — they're symptoms of a recognized medical condition that responds well to care.

What Is GSM?

Genitourinary syndrome of menopause is an umbrella term for the full range of changes to the vulva, vagina, bladder, and urethra that result from the loss of estrogen (and testosterone) during and after menopause. The term was formally adopted in 2014 by the North American Menopause Society (NAMS) and the International Society for the Study of Women's Sexual Health (ISSWSH) to replace the older term "vaginal atrophy."[1]

That change in terminology matters. "Vaginal atrophy" was clinical-sounding, carried an air of irreversibility, and — most importantly — described only one part of a much broader condition. The "genitourinary" in GSM reflects something important: this is not just a vaginal condition. It affects the entire lower urogenital system, including the bladder and urethra. Women can experience symptoms in any or all of three categories:

  • Vaginal and vulvar symptoms — dryness, irritation, burning, itching, tissue fragility
  • Sexual symptoms — pain with intercourse, reduced lubrication and sensation, avoidance of intimacy
  • Urinary symptoms — urgency, urge incontinence, stress incontinence, recurrent UTIs, increased frequency, nocturia

Not every woman experiences all three categories with equal severity. Some have primarily urinary symptoms. Some have primarily vaginal and sexual symptoms. Many have a mix. What they share is the same underlying cause.

Why Hormones Matter So Much Down There

Estrogen receptors are densely concentrated throughout the vaginal walls, vulvar tissue, urethra, bladder neck, and pelvic floor muscles. Testosterone receptors are also present throughout this tissue — a fact that is frequently overlooked, but clinically important. When both hormones decline (estrogen drops sharply at menopause; testosterone declines more gradually, beginning in a woman's 30s), the affected tissues change in predictable ways:[5]

  • Vaginal walls thin and lose their natural folds (rugae), reducing cushioning and lubrication capacity
  • Vulvar tissue loses elasticity and collagen, becoming more fragile and prone to irritation
  • The urethra thins and loses its ability to seal tightly, making it harder to prevent leakage under pressure
  • The bladder lining becomes more sensitive and irritable, triggering urgency signals even when the bladder isn't full
  • Pelvic floor muscles lose hormonal support for maintaining tone, reducing their capacity to resist pressure spikes

These changes are progressive. Unlike hot flashes — which often improve as the body adjusts to lower estrogen — the tissue and muscle changes of GSM don't plateau or self-correct. They worsen as time goes on, which is why women who dismiss early symptoms often find them significantly more disruptive years later.

The Full Symptom Picture

One of the reasons GSM stays so underdiagnosed is that its symptoms can seem disconnected. A woman might mention vaginal dryness to her gynecologist and bladder urgency to a urologist without anyone ever connecting the two as part of the same condition. Here's what the full picture looks like:

Vaginal and Vulvar Symptoms

  • Dryness, irritation, and burning — at rest or with activity
  • Itching or a persistent sense of discomfort
  • Loss of the natural folds in the vaginal walls, which reduces cushioning
  • Increased susceptibility to minor tears or abrasions from normal activity
  • Discharge changes — including watery discharge from inflammation

Sexual Symptoms

  • Pain during intercourse (dyspareunia) — often from tissue thinning and dryness
  • Reduced sensation or arousal
  • Difficulty with lubrication even when aroused
  • Anticipatory avoidance of intimacy due to expected pain

Urinary Symptoms

  • Urinary urgency — sudden, intense need to urinate that's difficult to defer
  • Urge incontinence — leaking before reaching the bathroom
  • Stress incontinence — leaking with coughing, laughing, sneezing, or exercise
  • Increased urinary frequency — needing to urinate more often throughout the day
  • Recurrent UTIs — thinned urethral and vaginal tissue is more vulnerable to bacterial entry and colonization[3]
  • Nocturia — waking at night one or more times to urinate

It's worth emphasizing: women with GSM often don't connect these different symptoms as part of the same condition. The vaginal discomfort and the bladder leaks feel unrelated. They may see entirely different providers for each without the full picture ever being assembled. The name GSM exists precisely to make that connection explicit.

Why Most Women Have Never Heard of It

The underdiagnosis of GSM isn't a mystery — it has several compounding causes.

The old terminology created a narrow frame. "Vaginal atrophy" sounded like a vaginal problem only. It didn't prompt anyone to ask about bladder symptoms or UTIs. And the word "atrophy" carried connotations of permanent, irreversible decline — which discouraged both patients and providers from pursuing treatment.

GSM as a term is only about a decade old. It has not yet fully permeated into routine primary care practice. Many physicians — particularly outside of OB-GYN and urology — are not screening for it systematically.

Patients don't bring it up. Surveys show that fewer than 1 in 4 women with GSM symptoms have discussed them with a doctor.[2] Embarrassment plays a role. So does the widespread assumption that these symptoms are simply part of getting older — something to be managed, not treated.

The result is a treatment gap. Millions of women are managing GSM symptoms with absorbent pads, restricting their activities, avoiding intimacy, and assuming this is simply how life is now. That's a real cost — to quality of life, to relationships, to confidence — and it's largely unnecessary. Effective treatments exist.

Local Hormone Therapy — The Most Targeted Treatment

When it comes to addressing the tissue changes of GSM, local vaginal estrogen is the most directly targeted treatment available. It comes in several forms — cream, vaginal ring, and tablet or suppository — and delivers low-dose estrogen exactly where the tissue changes are occurring.

The key distinction from systemic hormone replacement therapy (HRT) is absorption. Local estrogen stays local. Blood levels remain very low — far lower than with pills, patches, or injections. This is not a minor technical detail: it means local estrogen has a fundamentally different safety profile from systemic HRT, and is considered appropriate for most women, including many who cannot or choose not to use systemic hormones.[5] If you've been told you can't do HRT, that does not automatically mean you can't use local vaginal estrogen — they are different treatments.

What local estrogen does:

  • Restores thickness and elasticity to vaginal and urethral tissue
  • Reduces bladder hypersensitivity and urgency
  • Reduces recurrent UTIs by restoring the protective tissue environment
  • Improves natural lubrication and reduces pain with intercourse
  • Supports the tissue environment in which the pelvic floor operates

Full effects typically take 4 to 12 weeks to develop. Ongoing use is required — stopping allows symptoms to return, because the underlying hormonal deficit doesn't change. Local estrogen is a maintenance treatment, not a one-time fix.

It also bears mentioning that testosterone receptors are present throughout the urogenital tissue affected by GSM. Vaginal testosterone — and DHEA (sold under the brand name Prasterone), which converts to both estrogen and testosterone locally — are also being used to address the testosterone-receptor component of GSM. If you're interested in these options, a discussion with a gynecologist or menopause specialist is the right starting point.

Women interested in local hormone therapy should speak with their OB-GYN, primary care provider, or a menopause specialist. NAMS maintains a provider finder at menopause.org for those looking for a clinician with specific expertise in this area.

What Local Hormones Don't Fix

Local estrogen is excellent at restoring tissue health and reducing urgency, frequency, and recurrent UTIs. It does not, however, rebuild pelvic floor muscle strength that has been lost.

This distinction matters for understanding which symptoms respond to which treatments. Stress incontinence — the leaking that happens with coughing, laughing, sneezing, or exercise — is primarily a muscle problem. The pelvic floor muscles are responsible for resisting the sudden pressure spikes that these activities create. When those muscles have weakened, the urethra cannot stay closed against the force. No amount of tissue restoration fixes a muscle that isn't strong enough to do its job.

Women with GSM who have significant stress incontinence, or who have mixed incontinence (both urge and stress components), often need pelvic floor muscle rehabilitation in addition to hormonal treatment. Treating only the tissue side leaves the muscle side unaddressed — and vice versa.

Pelvic Floor Therapy as the Complement to Hormone Treatment

This is where treatments that directly rehabilitate pelvic floor muscle strength become essential. Emsella uses high-intensity focused electromagnetic (HIFEM) technology to trigger 11,200 pelvic floor muscle contractions per 28-minute session — a level of muscle activation that is not achievable through voluntary exercise, regardless of how consistently or correctly Kegel exercises are performed.[4]

For women with GSM, the combination of local hormone therapy and Emsella addresses both components of the condition in parallel:

  • Local estrogen restores the tissue environment — reducing bladder sensitivity, urgency, recurrent UTIs, and vaginal discomfort
  • Emsella restores pelvic floor muscle strength — reducing stress incontinence and supporting urge incontinence management

Neither treatment alone is as comprehensive as both together for women who have significant incontinence alongside the tissue changes of GSM. The two approaches are complementary, not competing — and can be pursued simultaneously.

Emsella works regardless of estrogen levels. Because it activates muscle fibers directly through electromagnetic energy rather than relying on neurological recruitment pathways that hormones support, it bypasses the limitation that makes voluntary pelvic floor exercise less effective in postmenopausal women. The muscles contract because the device makes them contract — not because hormones are present to facilitate it.

Clinical studies show 95% of Emsella patients report significant improvement in urinary incontinence symptoms.[4] The standard protocol is six sessions over three weeks, 28 minutes per session, with no preparation needed and no downtime afterward.

Having the Conversation with Your Doctor

One of the most practical things this post can offer is language. Many women don't bring up these symptoms, and many providers don't ask. Naming the condition can change the nature of the conversation.

You can say: "I've been reading about genitourinary syndrome of menopause — could my symptoms be related to GSM?"

You can ask specifically: "Is local vaginal estrogen something I'd be a candidate for?"

If you've been told you can't use HRT, follow up with: "I understand that's different from local vaginal estrogen — can we talk about whether that would be appropriate for me?"

If your symptoms are being dismissed as "just part of getting older," it may be worth seeking a second opinion from a gynecologist or a menopause specialist. NAMS (the North American Menopause Society) maintains a directory of certified menopause practitioners at menopause.org. These are providers who have specific expertise in exactly this area.

You are not overreacting. You are not asking for too much. These are real symptoms with real treatments, and you deserve a provider who takes them seriously.

The Takeaway

GSM is common — affecting up to 84% of postmenopausal women. It is progressive — unlike hot flashes, it does not improve without treatment. And it is undertreated — not because treatments don't exist, but because millions of women have never been told there's a name for what they're experiencing.

The condition has two main treatment targets:

  • The tissue changes — thinning, loss of elasticity, bladder hypersensitivity — are addressed by local hormone therapy (local estrogen, or DHEA/prasterone)
  • The muscle weakness — stress incontinence, reduced pelvic floor tone — is addressed by pelvic floor rehabilitation such as Emsella

Knowing the name — GSM — is often the first step. It reframes what feels like "aging" as a medical condition with a mechanism and a treatment path. You don't have to manage this alone with pads and avoidance. You don't have to restrict your activities or avoid intimacy. And you don't have to wait until symptoms are significantly worse to seek care.
